The momentum behind backyard housing in Central Florida has never been stronger. Whether you are tapping into [long-term rental income](/blog/florida-adu-rental-income-opportunity) or designing a multi-generational family compound, building an [Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU)](/blog/what-is-an-adu-florida) is a profound way to maximize your real estate asset.

Moving from a conceptual dream to an active, permitted construction site requires strict operational planning. At Prefabricated.co, the most critical phases happen long before a shovel touches dirt.

These are the verification milestones to run through before you sign with a builder or purchase a modular unit. [Download the full interactive Florida ADU Starter Kit](/free-adu-course#starter-kit) for budget sheets, planning workbooks, and builder questionnaires.

## Checklist 1: The Local Municipal Verification

Your project is bound by local building and zoning rules. Never assume a general contractor knows your parcel restrictions.

* **Jurisdiction check:** Confirm via the county property appraiser whether you are under [City of Orlando by-right rules](/blog/city-orlando-vs-unincorporated-orange-county) or unincorporated Orange County guidelines (Special Exception via the BZA).
* **The setback map:** What are your exact rear, side, and front setbacks? Do you have a certified boundary survey showing these lines?
* **Lot coverage limits:** Does your municipality cap impervious surface percentage (concrete, roofs, driveways)? Will the ADU push you over the threshold?
* **HOA clearance:** If you are in an HOA, have you reviewed CC&Rs and submitted an architectural review application?

## Checklist 2: Site Infrastructure and Utility Auditing

Connecting utilities to a secondary structure is where hidden costs lurk—model them in your [ADU budget worksheet](/blog/florida-adu-budget-worksheet) before you commit.

* **Main electrical capacity:** Is your primary panel rated for at least 200 amps? If it is 100 amps, have you budgeted a main service upgrade with your utility provider?
* **Septic/sewer evaluation:** On city sewer, is the secondary lateral tie-in lower than the ADU foundation for gravity flow? On septic, has the health department signed off on capacity for an additional dwelling?
* **Equipment access:** Is there a clear path at least 10 feet wide for excavators and trenchers? For modular delivery, are overhead power lines or heritage oaks blocking a crane?

## Checklist 3: The 4 Critical Questions for Your Builder

When interviewing contractors or modular manufacturers, look for professionals who understand Central Florida's biome. Never sign without asking:

### 1. "Is this structure engineered to meet current Florida Building Code wind-load requirements?"

Your ADU must withstand severe hurricane-force winds (typically 130–150 mph depending on coastal distance). Demand site-specific structural plans detailing hurricane straps, ties, and anchor bolts.

### 2. "What specific insulation envelope package are you deploying?"

Standard fiberglass batts invite high energy bills and mold in humid climates. Demand closed-cell spray foam or continuous rigid exterior insulation—as outlined in [staged resilience](/blog/staged-resilience-efficiency-before-solar-panels).

### 3. "Are windows and glass doors impact-rated with Low-E coatings?"

Double-pane hurricane impact glass with Low-E coating reflects solar heat while meeting [Florida Building Code](https://www.floridabuilding.org/) glazing requirements.

### 4. "How are local building inspections managed?"

For factory-built modular units, verify a Florida DBPR approval stamp so the structure is pre-inspected at the factory—local inspectors focus on site prep, foundation, and utility hookups, shortening your timeline.
