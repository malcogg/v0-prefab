Water security is one of the defining challenges of modern housing. Aging municipal infrastructure, population growth, and weather that swings from drought to deluge make single-pipe dependence a real vulnerability.

At [Prefabricated.co](/), resilient infrastructure is core to the [EarthNest model](/earthnest-living-systems). For a small-footprint home or backyard [ADU](/blog/what-is-an-adu-florida), rainwater should be planned at design—not bolted on after occupancy.

Tiny homes have compact, durable rooflines optimized for catchment. Safe, high-volume systems require engineering discipline. Before you buy tanks or run plumbing, here is what to know first. For ADU-scale wet-season design and food-forest integration, also read our deep dive: [Designing for the Deluge](/blog/rainwater-harvesting-low-footprint-resilience).

## THOW vs. permanent ADU: same roof math, different code path

Travel trailers and THOWs may harvest rain for irrigation, but **potable use, permanent plumbing, and occupancy** fall under different rules than foundation-built [ADUs](/blog/what-is-an-adu-florida). Treat this article as mechanical primer for any small roof; confirm legal use with [county ADU rules](/adu-rules) before you pipe harvested water to interior fixtures.

Our ADU-focused guide uses the **600-gallon-per-inch** shorthand for larger modular roof footprints (~1,000 sq ft equivalent). Tiny-home math above uses 0.62 gal/sq ft—both are correct; pick the formula that matches your plan set dimensions.

## The Foundation of Catchment Math

The governing formula: **For every 1 inch of rain on 1 square foot of roof, you collect approximately 0.62 gallons.**

Example for a compact tiny home:

* Roof footprint: **500 square feet**
* Rainfall: **1 inch** in a single storm
* **500 × 0.62 = 310 gallons** in minutes

Central Florida averages [over 50 inches of rain annually](https://climatecenter.fsu.edu/products-services/data/other/climate-normals)—a 500 sq ft roof can harvest **15,000+ gallons per year**. A 50-gallon decorative barrel overflows almost instantly in a subtropical downpour. Scale to IBC totes or dedicated cisterns (500–2,500 gallons) matched to your [homestead irrigation zones](/blog/backyard-homesteading-small-lots-florida).

## The Mechanical Elements of a Safe System

Landscape irrigation, laundry, or potable use (where ordinances allow) should follow [American Rainwater Catchment Systems Association (ARCSA)](https://www.arcsa.org/) guidance. Four stages matter:

### 1. High-flow guttering and shielding

Upgrade to 6-inch seamless aluminum gutters and commercial downspouts for torrential rain. Micro-mesh guards block leaves and debris under tree canopy.

### 2. First-flush diverter

After dry spells, roofs collect pollen, dust, and droppings. The first wash is contaminated. A **first-flush diverter** on the downspout captures the initial flush (often ~10 gallons per 1,000 sq ft of roof) before clean flow enters storage.

### 3. UV-opaque storage

Translucent tanks plus Florida sun equals algae within days. Use dark, opaque cisterns or fully wrapped poly tanks.

### 4. High-volume overflow

When tanks fill during multi-day storms, route overflow away from foundations into mulch basins, French drains, or thirsty landscape features—never against the ADU pad.

### 5. Filtration and use tiers

Most Florida homeowners start with **irrigation-grade** harvest, then add sediment filtration and UV or carbon stages if pursuing laundry or potable tiers allowed locally. Document your intended use on permit drawings—inspectors treat potable rainwater differently from landscape supply. Cross-reference DEP guidance and your county health department before you label any tap "drinking."

## Sizing Your System for Resilience

Size storage against dry-season irrigation demand for your [food forest and raised beds](/blog/food-forests-gardens-greenhouses-small-properties), not just household taps. Pair catchment with [graywater diversion](/blog/core-systems-low-waste-tiny-home) so landscape hydration is not entirely roof-dependent.

Integrating rainwater in initial site plans protects plant investments during watering restrictions, lowers utility overhead, and supports [self-sufficient tiny living](/blog/tiny-home-self-sufficiency-decentralized-utilities). Download the [Florida Rainwater Resilience PDF](/blog/rainwater-harvesting-low-footprint-resilience#rainwater-guide-download) for filtration tiers and overflow routing checklists.

## Pumps, pressure, and maintenance

Gravity feeds irrigation on flat lots; multi-story ADUs or long lateral runs need a small transfer pump with dry-run protection. Install leaf screens you can reach without a ladder, and inspect first-flush diverters after pollen season—Central Florida's spring coating clogs neglected assemblies fast. Winterize exposed hose bibs on the rare freeze night; burst fittings waste stored water and damage foundations.

Log annual harvest volume against irrigation use. If tanks never fill, your roof area or gutter capacity is undersized; if they always overflow, storage or distribution is the bottleneck—not the climate.

When pairing with municipal water, install backflow prevention where code requires it—irrigation cross-connections are a common inspection failure on new ADU packages even when rainwater is landscape-only.

Store tanks uphill from beds when topography allows; otherwise plan pump duty cycles and power draw in your [solar load sheet](/blog/solar-battery-basics-tiny-home-living). Every foot of elevation you gain for free is electricity you never buy.

Mark tank level and overflow outlets on your site plan—emergency responders and future buyers should see water infrastructure as clearly as electric meters and condenser pads.

In freeze-prone microclimates (North Florida and inland cold pockets), insulate exposed lateral lines or drain outdoor plumbing during the handful of nights that threaten hard freeze—stored water is useless if delivery pipes split.

Label every valve and tank port at commissioning—future maintenance happens in storms, not on calm Tuesday afternoons.
