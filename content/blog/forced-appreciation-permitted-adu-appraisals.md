When evaluating a real estate investment, there are two distinct types of returns: short-term cash flow and long-term equity growth. While many homeowners focus entirely on the immediate monthly rental checks an [Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU)](/blog/what-is-an-adu-florida) can generate, the true, generational wealth of a backyard home is unlocked through a phenomenon known as **forced appreciation**.

Forced appreciation is the process of manually increasing a property's value by making physical, permanent improvements to the real estate. However, not all backyard structures are created equal in the eyes of a certified real estate appraiser.

At [Prefabricated.co](/), our core pillars bridge the gap between financial investment and legal compliance. Today, we dive into appraisal mechanics to explain why legally permitted ADUs built on permanent foundations outperform temporary units—such as Tiny Homes on Wheels (THOWs) or unpermitted sheds—when it comes to driving up your home's equity.

## How an Appraiser Views Your Backyard

To understand why permanence matters, look at your property through the lens of a licensed real estate appraiser working under the [Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice (USPAP)](https://www.appraisalfoundation.org/improve/uspap).

When an appraiser evaluates a property, they classify every feature into one of two categories:

1. **Real property:** Land and anything permanently attached to it, such as a primary house, a swimming pool, or a permitted detached ADU built on a concrete foundation.
2. **Personal property:** Movable assets that are not legally bound to the land, such as furniture, vehicles, above-ground pools, and Tiny Homes on Wheels.

This classification forms a hard boundary. Real property adds directly to the appraised valuation of your real estate asset. Personal property is excluded from the home's real estate appraisal value, regardless of how much it cost to purchase or how beautiful it looks.

## The Valuation Trajectory: Real Estate vs. Depreciating Assets

The financial trajectory of a permitted, permanent ADU and a temporary unit could not be more different.

### 1. The Permitted ADU (Appreciating Asset)

Because a permanent ADU is legally permitted and built to the strict [Florida Building Code](https://www.floridabuilding.org/), it represents an expansion of the property's total heated and cooled living space. When a neighborhood experiences market appreciation, your ADU appreciates right along with it. It acts as a force multiplier for equity. If local real estate values rise by 5% annually, that percentage applies to the newly elevated total valuation of your property compound—as we outline in [Running the Numbers](/blog/florida-adu-budget-worksheet) when you model total project cost against long-term value.

### 2. The Temporary Unit or THOW (Depreciating Asset)

A Tiny Home on Wheels is built on a trailer chassis and comes with a vehicular title or registration rather than a real estate deed. Because it is on wheels, it is a depreciating asset—behaving financially like a car or recreational vehicle (RV). The moment you park a THOW in your backyard, its market value begins to decrease over time.

Furthermore, if you sell your home, a temporary unit cannot be included in the buyer's traditional 30-year mortgage financing, forcing you to either sell the unit separately or discount the deal to move it. THOWs can still fit dedicated [tiny home communities](/tiny-home-communities)—but on a standard residential lot they are a different asset class than a permitted ADU.

## The "Comparable Sales" Conundrum

When determining your home's value, appraisers rely heavily on the Sales Comparison Approach. They look for recent sales of similar properties within a tight geographic radius—typically matching bedroom count, bathroom count, and total square footage.

* **With a permitted ADU:** The appraiser pulls comps of other properties that feature legal secondary units. They apply a dollar-amount adjustment for the secondary living space, directly elevating your home's baseline value. This provides a clean, justifiable bump in equity that banks trust for refinancing or home sales.
* **With an unpermitted or temporary unit:** Because the unit isn't legally tied to the land or recognized by the county property appraiser, the appraiser cannot give it a positive structural adjustment. If an unpermitted structure violates local zoning setbacks or lacks proper utility engineering, it can be flagged as a **detriment to value**. The appraiser may subtract value to account for the cost a future buyer would face to remove the non-compliant structure—another reason to follow the [7-step legal build path](/blog/seven-steps-legal-backyard-home) and confirm jurisdiction early via [City of Orlando vs. unincorporated county](/blog/city-orlando-vs-unincorporated-orange-county) rules.

## Unlocking Capital: Refinancing and Future Leverage

The real estate equity gained through forced appreciation isn't just numbers on a page—it is leverage you can use to build further wealth.

When you increase your property's value with a legally permitted ADU, your loan-to-value (LTV) ratio drops. That opens the door for a cash-out refinance or a [Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC)](/blog/florida-adu-budget-worksheet)—capital you can use for your next investment, a [closed-loop homestead](/closed-loop-homestead) upgrade, or off-grid infrastructure.

With a temporary unit, that capital stays locked. You spent money on the unit, but the bank behaves as if your backyard is empty from an appraisal standpoint.
