When you decide to build an Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU) in Central Florida, one of the first things you will notice is that everyone talks about the "Orlando market." But if you want to stay legally compliant, you have to stop thinking about your market as a single city. You need to look closely at your tax bill, your zoning map, and your local government.

A massive portion of the land that people call "Orlando" is actually governed by two completely separate local governments: the **City of Orlando** and **Unincorporated Orange County**.

At [Prefabricated.co](/), we focus heavily on navigating the legal and permitting path. If you build a backyard home under the wrong set of rules, you risk facing expensive code violations, or worse, being forced to tear the structure down.

Let's break down the major differences between the City of Orlando and Unincorporated Orange County so you know exactly which rulebook applies to your backyard.

## How to Determine Your True Jurisdiction

Before comparing the rules, you have to figure out who collects your property taxes and issues your building permits. You cannot rely on your mailing address. Millions of homeowners have an address that says "Orlando, FL," but they actually live outside city limits under county rule.

To find out where you stand, use local government lookups:

* Go to the [Orange County Property Appraiser (OCPA)](https://www.ocpafl.org/) website and look up your parcel.
* Look at the **Tax District** or **Municipality** field.
* If it says *City of Orlando*, you follow the city's municipal code.
* If it says *Orange County* or *Unincorporated*, you follow the county's code.

When in doubt, confirm with a feasibility review before you order plans—see [7 steps to a legal backyard home](/blog/seven-steps-legal-backyard-home) for where this fits in the overall timeline.

## The Permitting Framework: By-Right vs. Special Exception

The biggest philosophical difference between the city and the county is how they view the right to build an ADU. For background on what an ADU is, start with [What is an ADU?](/blog/what-is-an-adu-florida).

### City of Orlando: Permitted "By-Right"

The City of Orlando treats ADUs as a valuable tool to fight housing shortages. In most single-family residential zones within city limits, ADUs are allowed **By-Right**. This means if your site plan matches the dimensional requirements (setbacks, height, and square footage), the city *must* approve your building permit. There are no neighbor notifications, no public debates, and no special permission boards required. It is a purely administrative process handled by city staff.

### Unincorporated Orange County: The "Special Exception" Path

Orange County handles ADUs with a lot more caution to protect the traditional feel of suburban neighborhoods. In the county's standard residential zones, a detached backyard home with a full kitchen is usually classified as requiring a **[Special Exception](/blog/special-exception-orange-county-zoning)**. This means you cannot just submit architectural plans and start pouring concrete. You have to apply to the Board of Zoning Adjustment (BZA), notify every neighbor within a few hundred feet, post public signs in your yard, and pitch your project at a public hearing.

## Setbacks and Placement: Where Can It Sit?

Once you know your framework, you have to look at your physical property lines. Setbacks dictate exactly where on your lot a structure can legally sit.

**City of Orlando (typical detached ADU):**

* **Primary placement:** Side or rear yard only; cannot sit closer to the street than the main house.
* **Rear setback:** Typically **5 feet** for a single-story detached unit.
* **Side setback:** Typically **5 feet** for standard single-story configurations.

**Unincorporated Orange County (typical detached ADU):**

* **Primary placement:** Rear yard preferred; must respect rigid residential side/rear boundary lines.
* **Rear setback:** Typically matches the primary home's setback (often **20 to 25 feet**), unless granted a specific variance or exception.
* **Side setback:** Matches primary home standards (often **5 to 7.5 feet** depending on the specific zoning district).

As you can see, the City of Orlando is much more relaxed when it comes to setbacks, allowing you to place a modular or site-built ADU closer to your property lines to maximize your usable yard space. In the county, a deep backyard is almost always required just to satisfy the rear setback rules.

Always verify current code on your parcel—county and city rules change; use [ADU rules by county](/adu-rules) as your hub and confirm with the AHJ. For county zoning context, see [Orange County Growth Management](https://www.ocfl.net/PlanningDevelopment/GrowthManagement.aspx).

## Utility Constraints: The Hidden Expense

You can build the most beautiful, energy-efficient [prefabricated modular ADU](/earthnest-living-systems) in the world, but it is nothing more than an expensive storage shed if you cannot connect it to utilities. How you connect those utilities depends entirely on your jurisdiction.

### Water and Sewer Connections

* **City of Orlando:** The city requires ADUs to hook into the primary home's existing water meter and sewer lateral whenever possible. This keeps impact fees lower because you aren't establishing a brand-new utility account with the city.
* **Orange County:** The county has strict rules regarding capacity, especially if your property relies on a septic tank system. If you are on septic, the [Florida Department of Health](https://www.floridahealth.gov/) will require you to prove your tank is large enough to handle the extra gallonage of an ADU. If it isn't, you will be forced to upgrade your entire septic system, which can add $8,000 to $15,000 to your budget before construction even starts.

### Electrical Subpanels

Both jurisdictions require compliance with national standards, meaning all electrical work must follow the [National Electrical Code (NEC)](https://www.nfpa.org/codes-and-standards/all-codes-and-standards/list-of-codes-and-standards/detail?code=70). You can run power to your ADU from a subpanel tied to your main house meter, but your existing electrical panel must have the capacity (typically requiring a 200-amp main service to safely feed a 100-amp subpanel for the ADU). If your primary home only has a 100-amp or 150-amp service, you will need to pay your local utility provider (such as OUC or Duke Energy) for a main service upgrade.

## Size Limitations

How big can your backyard home be? Both jurisdictions limit the size of the ADU based on the size of your primary house, but the math is different.

* **City of Orlando:** An ADU cannot exceed 40% of the gross floor area of the primary home, or 1,200 square feet, whichever is *less*.
* **Orange County:** The county limits the living area of an ADU to 30% of the primary home's living area, or 1,000 square feet, whichever is *less*.

This means if your main house is 2,000 square feet, the city would allow you to build an ADU up to 800 square feet. In the county, that same main house limits your ADU to just 600 square feet.

## Making the Right Choice for Your Property

Navigating these differences can feel overwhelming, but clarity is power. If you live in the City of Orlando, your path is faster, predictable, and allows for a larger footprint closer to your property lines. If you live in Unincorporated Orange County, you must prepare for a longer, public approval process that requires careful attention to setbacks and septic capacity.

No matter where your lot sits, building to the strict Florida Building Code on a permanent foundation ensures that your project adds long-term value, equity, and rental potential to your real estate portfolio.
