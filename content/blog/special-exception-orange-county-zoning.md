## The Jurisdictional Divide: City vs. County

Before we dive into the Special Exception, we must clarify a vital geographic distinction. Your mailing address might say "Orlando, FL," but that does not necessarily mean you are governed by the City of Orlando. A massive percentage of the greater Orlando area actually falls under the jurisdiction of **unincorporated Orange County**.

Why does this matter? Because the City of Orlando and Orange County have radically different approaches to ADUs.

The City of Orlando has made significant strides in recent years to encourage ADU development. In many city zoning districts, ADUs are permitted "By-Right." By-Right means that as long as your plans meet the objective standards (size limits, setbacks, height restrictions, and permanent foundation requirements), the building department *must* approve your permit. There are no public hearings and no subjective debates.

Orange County, however, takes a more protective approach to its single-family neighborhoods. In most of unincorporated Orange County's residential zones (like R-1, R-1A, etc.), a detached ADU with a full kitchen is **not** a By-Right use. Instead, it is classified as a use that requires a "Special Exception."

## What Exactly is a Special Exception?

A Special Exception is a formal permission granted by the local zoning authority to use property in a way that is not explicitly allowed "By-Right" in that specific zoning district, but which may be permissible if certain conditions are met.

Think of it this way: The county is saying, *"We don't automatically allow backyard apartments here because we want to preserve the low-density character of the neighborhood. However, we recognize that every property is unique. If you can prove that your ADU won't negatively impact your neighbors, we might grant you an exception."*

This is a critical pivot in your project timeline. A By-Right permit is an administrative process (handled by county staff). A Special Exception is a **public, quasi-judicial process**.

## The Board of Zoning Adjustment (BZA) Process

To obtain a Special Exception in Orange County, you must present your case to the Board of Zoning Adjustment (BZA). This process requires preparation, patience, and a thick skin. Here is what the timeline generally looks like:

### 1. The Pre-Application Meeting

Before you formally apply, you will meet with county planners to discuss your concept. They will review your site plan, your lot size, and the proposed size of the ADU. This is where working with Prefabricated.co pays dividends; bringing professional, code-compliant modular plans to this meeting shows the county you are serious and prepared.

### 2. The Application and Fees

If the planners give you a green light to proceed, you will file a formal application. This involves submitting detailed site plans, elevations, and a written justification explaining why your ADU meets the county's criteria for a Special Exception (e.g., it is architecturally compatible, it won't cause traffic issues, it respects setbacks). You will also pay a non-refundable application fee.

### 3. Public Notification

This is the part that catches many homeowners off guard. Because a Special Exception affects the neighborhood, the county requires you to notify your neighbors. Signs will be posted on your property, and letters will be mailed to property owners within a certain radius (often 300 to 500 feet). Your neighbors will be given the date of your BZA hearing and the opportunity to voice their support or opposition.

### 4. The Public Hearing

On the day of the hearing, you (or your representative) will stand before the BZA to present your case. County staff will provide their recommendation, and then the floor will be opened for public comment. If your neighbors object—perhaps citing concerns over parking, privacy, or neighborhood character—the BZA will take those objections into heavy consideration.

The BZA ultimately votes to approve, approve with conditions, or deny your request.

## The Homestead Requirement: Keeping it Local

If you successfully navigate the BZA and are granted your Special Exception, you will likely encounter another significant regulatory hurdle: the owner-occupancy or "Homestead" requirement.

In many jurisdictions that strictly regulate ADUs, local governments want to prevent single-family neighborhoods from being bought up by corporate investors and turned into multi-family rental compounds. To prevent this, they attach a strict condition to the Special Exception: **The property owner must reside on the property.**

In Florida, this is usually verified via the Homestead Exemption. To legally rent out your newly built ADU, you must claim the primary house as your primary residence and hold the Homestead Exemption on the county tax rolls. If you sell the property in the future, the Special Exception may be voided if the new owner does not also live on-site, or the new owner may have to re-apply.

For the traditional homeowner looking to house an aging parent, a returning college graduate, or seeking a $2,500/month rental income to offset their own mortgage, this requirement is a non-issue. But for out-of-state investors looking for pure rental arbitrage, it is a hard stop.
