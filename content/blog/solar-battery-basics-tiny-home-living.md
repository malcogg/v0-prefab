Generating your own power is liberating. For many minimalists, the tiny-home dream includes untethering from the grid—eliminating monthly electric bills and buffering regional outages.

Off-grid living requires systems engineering, not consumer impulse. You cannot buy random panels and batteries online and expect seamless residential performance.

## Sizing Your Solar System: The Order of Operations

The largest mistake is sizing solar to match a wasteful envelope. Poor insulation and air leaks force air conditioning to run continuously—requiring a massive, expensive array.

Follow strict order: **reduce baseline load before buying panels.**

Use advanced envelope techniques—[closed-cell spray foam on steel shells](/blog/container-home-living-system-thermal-envelope), sealed penetrations, and [inverter-driven mini-split HVAC](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/ductless-mini-split-air-conditioners)—to drop daily kWh demand. Read [Staged Resilience: Efficiency Before Solar](/blog/staged-resilience-efficiency-before-solar-panels) before you spec PV.

When demand falls, array cost falls proportionally. Run dwelling-side economics through the [ADU calculator](/adu-calculator) while you model energy independence—rent and utility stacks interact on small lots.

## Grid-tied, hybrid, and fully off-grid paths

Not every tiny home needs total grid severance. Many Florida ADU projects use **grid-tied solar** with net metering where utilities allow—lower battery cost, simpler maintenance, still meaningful bill reduction. **Hybrid systems** add a modest LiFePO4 bank for backup circuits (refrigeration, fans, lighting) during outages without sizing batteries for full HVAC.

Fully off-grid makes sense when utility extension costs are extreme or resilience is the primary design driver—but Florida humidity means AC load dominates. Be honest about overnight cooling demand when you talk to installers; undersized banks fail quietly on the first heat wave.

## The Four Core Components of an Off-Grid System

### 1. Photovoltaic (PV) array

Panels convert sunlight to raw DC electricity. Mount on the roof or on ground racks in a sunny clearing if canopy shade blocks the structure.

### 2. Solar charge controller

Panel output fluctuates with clouds and time of day. A **charge controller** (MPPT preferred) regulates voltage and current so batteries charge safely.

### 3. Battery bank

Batteries store daytime DC for nighttime loads. Modern residential off-grid favors **lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4)** over lead-acid: longer cycle life, deeper discharge without damage, minimal maintenance, and safer indoor installation in mechanical closets when code allows.

### 4. Power inverter

Batteries store DC; most appliances need AC. An **inverter** converts stored DC to stable 120V/240V AC for household loads.

## Peak Demand vs. Storage Capacity

Engineering requires separating **peak demand (kW)** from **storage capacity (kWh)**.

* **Peak demand:** Maximum instantaneous draw. Running dryer, electric water heater, and oven together requires an inverter sized for combined load.
* **Storage capacity:** Total energy the bank holds. Track daily consumption and add **days of autonomy**—reserve for consecutive overcast storm days common in Florida.

Grid-tied ADUs may use net metering instead of full off-grid stacks; jurisdiction and utility rules vary. Confirm paths through [ADU rules](/adu-rules) and a [site evaluation](/qualify) before you commit capital to batteries.

Document **peak vs. continuous** loads on a simple spreadsheet: refrigerator startup surge, mini-split compressor, well or transfer pump, and any induction cooktop you refuse to give up. Installers size inverter and bank from that list—not from generic online kits.

## Maintenance and safety basics

Clean panels quarterly—pollen and mildew cut output silently in humid counties. Track state of charge weekly on lithium banks; BMS apps show when a cell group drifts. Keep mechanical closets ventilated; inverters and chargers produce heat. Label AC and DC disconnects for first responders. Florida lightning makes whole-house surge protection on AC service and SPDs on DC runs cheap insurance compared with fried equipment after a nearby strike.

Treat solar like roofing: inspect mounts after named storms, re-torque where engineers specify, and keep vegetation from shading panels that were clear at install. A shaded string can drag whole-array performance down without tripping obvious alarms.

## Working with installers and code officials

Bring load calculations, equipment cut sheets, and single-line diagrams to permit review—inspectors approve systems they can trace on paper. Ask whether battery storage indoors triggers additional fire-code clearances in your jurisdiction. If you are renting the ADU, clarify who maintains panels and who carries insurance on attached arrays—ownership and lease terms should match the interconnection agreement.

Compare payback against utility rate escalators over ten years—not just today's bill. A tight envelope plus modest array often beats a oversized array on a leaky tiny shell; the [regenerative series hub](/blog/what-is-regenerative-tiny-living) treats energy as one loop among water, soil, and code-aware shelter.

**Example daily load sketch (efficient tiny ADU):** mini-split cooling 4–8 kWh (seasonal), refrigeration 1 kWh, LED lighting and devices 1–2 kWh, water pump 0.3 kWh—roughly 6–12 kWh/day before laundry or cooking electrification. Size battery for 1.5–2 days of that band if you target storm autonomy, then add inverter headroom for compressor start surge.

Module wattage × peak sun hours × system losses gives a first-pass array size—your installer should model shade across seasons, not just a clear December noon snapshot on the roof.

If you stay grid-tied, understand export limits and time-of-use windows—battery economics in Florida shift with tariff design, not panel count alone.

Commissioning matters: test inverter failover, battery BMS alarms, and critical-load panel labeling before the first named storm—not after a week on backup power.

Document serial numbers and warranty contacts in your [Starter Kit](/free-adu-course#starter-kit) binder—replacement timelines for inverters and batteries are measured in years, not weeks.
